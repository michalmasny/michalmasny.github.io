Overview:

My main research area is normative ethics. I am particularly interested in population and procreation ethics. In my BPhil thesis, supervised by Jeff McMahan, I engaged with Parfit's last published attempts to avoid the Repugnant Conclusion and with the Non-Identity Problem under uncertainty.

I retain a strong interest in practical ethics. At Oxford, I researched the ethics of emerging technologies under the supervision of Anders Sandberg from the Future of Humanity Institute. In particular, I have written about the moral status of intelligent machines, automation of work, the use of predictive algorithms in the criminal justice system, cognitive enhancement, and existential risk.

In epistemology, I have thus far written about knowledge-how, skill, epistemic luck, and suspended judgment. My research in this area was supervised by Tim Williamson. I am increasingly interested in epistemological perspectives on ignorance and uncertainty, as well as the many interconnections between epistemology and ethics.

Papers:

(1) Friedman on Suspended Judgment. Synthese. (forthcoming).

In a series of articles, Jane Friedman argues that suspended judgment is a sui generis first-order question-directed attitude and that one suspends judgment on some matter if and only if one genuinely inquires into this matter. This paper responds to Friedman's arguments against reductive higher-order propositional accounts of this attitude and raises worries about the details of her positive claim that one suspends iff one inquires. It subsequently defends a novel reductive higher-order propositional account of suspended judgment.

(2) On Parfit's Wide Dual Person-Affecting Principle

In his posthumously published paper, Parfit presents a novel axiological principle - the Wide Dual Person-Affecting Principle. Parfit argues that this principle can avoid the Repugnant Conclusion, but he leaves many questions unanswered. This paper shows that Parfit's principle cannot avoid this conclusion, makes progress on the question of relative importance of individual and collective goodness, and evaluates different possible conceptions of invididual goodness in unequal outcomes.

(3) On Parfit's Imprecise Lexical View

In his penultimate paper, Parfit appeals to the relations of lexical betterness and imprecise equality to resist the Continuum, the Sequential Beningn Addition, and the Simple Benign Addition arguments for the Repugnant Conclusion. This paper presents a range of objections to this Imprecise Lexical View, evaluates different conceptions of evaluative imprecision, and concludes that this view is not a satisfactory response to the aforementioned arguments.

(4) A deontological solution to the Real-World Non-Identity Problem

The Real-World Non-Identity Problem is the problem of accounting for the moral reason we intuitively have to create an individual who is conditionally expected to have the better life, rather than another individual who is conditionally expected to have a worse, but nonetheless good, life. This paper argues that we have a moral reason to minimise the risk of creating an individual with a bad life, and that, in almost all real-world procreation cases, the better an individual's life is conditionally expected to be, the less likely she is to have a bad life.
